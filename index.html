Men Are Sorry — and committed to doing better.

Note: This space centers the people harmed by sexism and misogyny. Men are here to listen first, not dominate the conversation.

“Sorry” is the beginning, not the end.
These are concrete ways to turn apology into daily practice.

Step 1 · Listen

Believe people when they tell you what hurt them. No interruptions, no “not all men”, no debate.

Step 2 · Own it

Say what you did, why it was harmful, and what you’ll change. No excuses, no blame-shifting.

Step 3 · Learn

Do the work yourself — books, therapy, conversations, reflection — instead of asking others to teach you.

Step 4 · Repeat

Change is measured in consistent behavior over time, not one perfect apology.

Why this exists

“Men Are Sorry” is a statement of accountability, not a slogan. It’s a reminder that apologies are hollow if they’re not backed by listening, repair, and changed behavior.

The reality

Harm is everywhere, not just headlines

Gendered harm isn’t only the extreme stories that go viral. It’s the “jokes”, the pressure, the dismissals, the fear of not being believed, and the small daily cuts that pile up.

“It’s not one big moment. It’s the constant calculation of what feels safe.”

The gap

“I’m a good guy” vs. real safety

Many men see themselves as “good” while staying silent, laughing along, or minimizing harm. Intent matters less than impact — and impact is what we’re responsible for.

  • Not speaking up is still a choice.
  • Defensiveness blocks growth.
  • Comfort is not more important than safety.

The invitation

Move from guilt to responsibility

Guilt that never turns into action just recenters you. This space asks: What will you do differently — online, in private, with friends, at work, and in relationships?

“Being sorry isn’t who you are. It’s what you choose to do next.”

Our promises

These are the kinds of commitments men can make and remake daily. They’re not rules you’ll nail perfectly, but directions you keep steering toward — especially when you mess up.

Listening

I won’t argue with someone else’s experience

  • I will listen fully before responding.
  • I won’t make it about my feelings first.
  • I will believe people when they describe what felt unsafe or painful.
  • I’ll ask what repair is actually helpful — and respect no.

Accountability

I will name the harm without excuses

  • I will practice saying, “I did this. It was harmful because…”
  • I won’t blame alcohol, stress, or “miscommunication”.
  • I’ll accept that I don’t get to control whether my apology is accepted.

Action

I will act when other men cause harm

  • I’ll challenge sexist “jokes”, even when it’s awkward.
  • I’ll check in with people who might have been harmed.
  • I’ll bring this up with friends, family, and coworkers — not just strangers online.

Learning

I will keep learning without demanding free labor

  • I’ll seek out books, podcasts, and workshops on gender and power.
  • I won’t expect the people hurt to educate me step by step.
  • I’ll stay open to feedback, even when it stings.
Stories & reflections

These short, anonymized reflections show what apology plus change can start to look like. They’re not templates — they’re examples of men taking responsibility seriously.

Reflection

“Not the funny one anymore”

“I used to be the guy who ‘roasted’ everyone. A partner told me she dreaded group hangs because I always turned the spotlight on her. I stopped calling it ‘just jokes’ and started asking myself why I needed to make people small to feel interesting.”

Repair

“I apologized the long way”

“Instead of one big emotional apology, I asked what would actually help my ex feel safer. That meant respecting distance, paying for therapy I’d promised years ago, and not trying to prove I’d changed — just showing it quietly.”

Calling in

“It started in the group chat”

“I called out a friend’s ‘joke’ in our guys’ group chat. It was weirdly quiet. Later two of them messaged that they’d been uncomfortable for years but didn’t want to be ‘the serious one’. Turns out a lot of us were waiting for someone else to speak first.”

Maybe I really am incapable of giving you that. If I am, just know I really tried everything (NOT SAM).

If you feel like you’re happier without me in your life, and that it makes things easier and less stressful, just say the word and I’m gone. Wallahi, I’ll still be a ring‑a‑ling away if you ever need me.

I believe people gravitate towards you because you’re the light that never dims, and we’re all moths drawn to your spark. I know that light will never stop shining, no matter what, but I’m deeply scared that I might have made it flicker for even a millisecond. Intent or no intent, that always seems to be the case, and maybe it really is me — maybe I’m just not made for this. Maybe love and loving are things I’m not capable of doing properly.

But with you, I was the closest I’ve ever been to not feeling empty. You filled me right to the brim — it even overflowed sometimes — and you taught me so much about what it feels like to be cared for. Maybe that feeling is still a foreign language to me. Hopefully one day I can grow into the kind of person who can love as well as you do. I thought I was doing okay, but it seems I still have a long way to go.

I don’t know — maybe all the laughter, happiness and exhilaration really were enjoyable and memorable. I’m sorry, from the Men Organisation of Sorry‑land. (Not Sam).
